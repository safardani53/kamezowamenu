VW'S Grand California sleeps four comfortably and comes with that all-important toilet and shower

THANKS to the strangulated crooning of The Eagles, we’ve all heard of the Hotel California.

And a quick Google search reveals you can find more hotels called The Grand online than you can saddos sloshing hate and bile across social media.

Well, this right here is the VW Grand California — a hotel on wheels.

Sure, there’s no room service. And the khazi doubles as a shower.

But I’ve stayed in far dingier digs, and none of them had the ability to hit the open road at the drop of a handbrake.

Big brother to the Transporter-based California, the Crafter-based Grand California can sleep four comfortably and comes with that all-important toilet and shower.

Unless you don’t mind being called Swampy by your mates, or are prepared to get busy finding campsites with amenities, the baby California is hampered by the lack of a bathroom.

The Grand California comes ready for long trips to remote  places — which is why VW has dubbed it the “big camper for big journeys”.

The interior is where the magic happens, so that’s where we’ll start.

You can store 110 litres of water which can be used for a shower (but not drunk), and it has storage for 90 litres of waste water.

Handily, that can be removed by a quick and clean cartridge system — no slopping out latrines Full Metal Jacket-style. The bathroom is cramped but efficient, shower pressure acceptable. Just remember to turn the heater on in advance.

I learned that the hard way.

Fold-out bunk beds housed in the cavernous roof are big enough for a couple of kids or smaller adults — they’re around six foot in length.

NIGHTCLUB-BLUE AMBIENT LIGHTING

At the back of the wagon is a double bed for mum and dad... or whoever you choose as a  bedfellow off the beaten path.

I’m not here to judge.

A table slots over the two rear seats (which have seat belts and ISOFIX fittings) in seconds, and the front seats swivel fully to make a dining area. Calor Gas powers a double hob and there’s a 70-litre fridge with freezer  section, plus a sink.

You really could live in one of these — and to cough up the £70,000 asking price you might have to.

Slick touches include an iPad-style control system for battery use, climate, water temperature etc, plus there’s nightclub-blue ambient lighting and Bluetooth speakers in the living area.

So if it’s a party bus you’re looking for, this is your jam.

Every time I turned around I found a new cubby hole ready to swallow tins of beans or a pack of travel Scrabble.

In total there’s around 1,000 litres of storage space.

Don’t be put off by its size either — driving it is such a doddle even Brian Harvey would manage it. The 177hp diesel engine has been lifted straight from the Golf TDI and never makes life difficult, especially as it is mated to a seamless eight-speed auto.

You can even option it in AWD.

Many of you will be reading this while enjoying your bank holiday weekend in a caravan or motorhome.

It’s one of the UK’s most treasured pastimes, and rightly so.

There are more than a million so-called leisure vehicles in Britain, and this week is  National Camping and Caravanning Week, marking 100 years of caravans.

If you are one of the millions who love life on the road, you will love the Grand California.

EV REBELLION

THE robots are rising – electric cars are threatening to take over our towns and cities.

Well, the expensive and leafy enclaves of London, at least.

Thanks to the ULEZ zone – which is essentially a tax on anyone who can’t afford to buy a new car – interest in EVs across the UK surged in April and May.

It’s not just down to the green tax of course.

Movements like Extinction Rebellion  – which ground road networks to a halt with  its protests recently – have also played a part. And it’s probably no coincidence that Tesla’s first affordable car, the Model 3, finally opened its order books.

Research conducted by car-buying data specialist Sophus3 shows that online traffic to electric vehicles has risen 56 per cent since the beginning of April.

That’s against a backdrop of a fall in interest for EVs in the first quarter of this year.

Amazing what a bit of publicity can do, eh?

    ASK ALFIE: USED CAR SPECIALIST

    Q I HAVE a 64-plate VW Polo and need a bigger car. I’m a foster carer so can have up to three children in the back and I need a big boot for a buggy if needed. My budget is around £12,000.

    Debra

    A There’s a car that would really suit you, Debra, and it’s the Honda HR-V. Small road footprint but big on the inside. It’ll easily swallow family stuff and, as it’s a Honda, it will be as  reliable as a Swiss clock. Good, low-mileage used examples on a 66-plate can be found for about £11,000.

    Q CAN you please help? I need to choose a company car urgently. I do around 30,000 miles a year and can choose from a VW Tiguan, BMW X1, Lexus ES 300h, Mercedes C200 d or a C220 dAMG Line Premium automatic.

    Darren Graham

    A Some great cars to choose from on that list, Darren, but the C220 d is the obvious choice. That Merc 2-litre turbodiesel is a beaut and an AMG Line  Premium will be swimming in toys. The Lexus is a good bet for company tax breaks but I’d be veering for the Merc as the best choice there.

    NB: after I emailed the above advice to  Darren, he responded to say he was in a Merc dealership looking at the C220d! A wise choice sir.

    Q APART from a 4x4, which is the best small hatchback that will cope well in winter weather?

    Pam Thornhill

    A You are planning ahead! Any modern, front-wheel-drive hatchback with traction and electronic stability control, and a decent set of tyres too, should get you through the worst our cruddy weather can throw at you. However, if you want extra peace of mind, buy a Peugeot 2008 with Grip Control, a system specially designed to get the car through snowy conditions.
